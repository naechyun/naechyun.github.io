Research

Improving sequence alignment and reducing reference bias

Alignment is the first step for many genomics applications. Currently, people rely on a linear haploid reference genome such as GRCh37 and GRCh38 for this task. However, more than half of the "standard" reference genomes is from one individual, resulting in confounding of downstream results. A solution is replacing the linear reference with a graph data structure that contains genotypes collected from many individuals, aka graph genome. Our study, FORGe, presents a ranking strategy for the inclusion of population data. As an extension of FORGe, my first-author project, reference flow, uses a small number of additional references built using population data to improve the alignment results.

Publications: Chen et al. (2020, preprint), Pritt et al. (2018)
Presentation: RECOMB-Seq (Washington DC, USA, 2019), Genome Informatics (CSHL, USA, 2019)

Developing computational methods for biological and medical data

Many research areas have been adopting modern sequencing techonology for faster and more accurate analysis. I collaborate with researchers at Johns Hopkins University to predict chromatin accessibility using machine learning methods. Also, I work with collaborators at National Taiwan Univeristy to study immunogenomics such as the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) complex.

Presentation: JHU Chromatin Workshop (2019)

Designing efficient hardware architecture for computational genomics

With the enormous volume of genomics data, how to process it efficiently has become a challenging topic. One solution is using specialized hardware architecture based on FPGA or ASIC to make computing fast or power efficient. I designed algorithms to meet hardware requirements and developed architectures for efficient computing. My research includes short read and long read alignment, multiple sequence alignment, and assembly.

Publications: Liao et al. (2018), Chen et al. (2018), Lin et al. (2018), Chang et al. (2016), Liu et al. (2016), Chen et al. (2015)
Presentations: APCCAS (Jeju, Korea, 2016), BioCAS (Atlanta, USA, 2015)
