Research

Improving sequence alignment and reducing reference bias

Alignment is the first step for many genomics applications. Currently, people rely on a linear haploid reference genome such as GRCh38 for this task. However, more than half of the "standard" reference genome is sequenced from one individual. This results in "reference bias" that confounds downstream analyses.
A solution is to use a reference genome containing population information. Our study, FORGe, presents a ranking strategy for the inclusion of population data in a comprehensive graph genome. We also devlope levioSAM to perform fast lift-over of alignments that are based on a variant-aware reference. Further, the reference flow framework uses a small number of additional population-aware linear references to improve alignment.

  • Publications: Chen et al. (2021), Mun*, Chen*, Langmead (2021), Pritt, Chen, Langmead (2018)
  • Presentation: RECOMB-Seq (Washington DC, USA, 2019; poster), Genome Informatics (CSHL, USA, 2019; poster)
  • Software: reference flow, levioSAM, FORGe, bowtie-majref

Accurate variant calling utilizing population data and deep learning

Variant calling is a genomic data processing method to identify differences between a sample and the reference, which is usually done using sophisticated processing and statistical methods. DeepVariant is a deep-learning-based variant caller developed by Google Health. While interning on the DeepVariant team, I developed a population-aware DeepVariant model to incorporate population information in variant calling. This model is more accurate than the default DeepVariant model.

  • Publications: Chen et al. (2021, preprint)
  • Presentation: Biological Data Science (virtual, 2020; poster)
  • Software: DeepVariant

Developing computational methods for biological and medical data

Many research areas have been adopting modern sequencing techonology for faster and more accurate analysis. I collaborate with researchers at Johns Hopkins University to predict chromatin accessibility using machine learning methods. Also, I work with collaborators at National Taiwan Univeristy to study immunogenomics such as the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) complex and the Adaptive Immune Receptor Repertoire (AIRR).

  • Publications: Lin et al. (2021, preprint)
  • Presentation: JHU Chromatin Workshop (2019; poster)

Designing efficient hardware architecture for computational genomics

With the enormous volume of genomics data, how to process it efficiently has become a challenging topic. One solution is using specialized hardware architecture based on FPGA or ASIC to make computing efficient. I design specialized algorithms to meet hardware requirements (which are usually more limited than that of software) and develop architectures for efficient computing. My research includes short read and long read alignment, multiple sequence alignment, and assembly.

  • Selected publications: Liao et al. (2018), Chen et al. (2018), Chen et al. (2015)
  • Presentations: APCCAS (Jeju, Korea, 2016; oral), BioCAS (Atlanta, USA, 2015; poster)
